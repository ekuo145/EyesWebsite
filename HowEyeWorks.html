How the Eyes Work

Photoreceptors in the retina work with neurons to create action potentials that go to our brain's visual cortex.

But what is the full pathway from light being detected to when it reaches our brain?

Scroll Down to Continue!

What is Light?

When we see, what we actually see are electromagnetic rays at the wavelength of visible light (see figure 1 below for visualization).

Visible light falls from 400nm to 700nm, with the lower numbers representing bluer colors and the bigger numbers representing more red colors (Crash Course, 2015).

Fun fact: Even though we can see millions of shades of color, some colors like magenta don't actually exist, and our mind makes it up when our cones Photoreceptors that specialize in seeing color pick up pure red and pure blue

How the Sensory Pathway Start?

First, the photoreceptors detect light in a process called sensory reception (Campbell et al., 2020). Following this, they generate receptor potentials in a process called sensory transduction (Campbell et al., 2020).

The receptor potentials are like mini signals saying, there is something here, and if there is a big enough receptor potential, an action potential is released. Stronger stimulus leads to a bigger receptor potential which leads to more action potentials being produced(see figure 2 for a visualization) (Campbell et al., 2020). The action potentials then follow the axons of the ganglion neurons Neurons that take in info from photoreceptors and creates action potentials, its axons form the optic nerve. to the brain.

But how does the action potential stay around that long?

Enzyme-catalyzed reactions boost the energy of the stimulus so that it can be detected and processed by the nervous system better in a process known as amplification (Campbell et al., 2020). However, photoreceptors are numbed in a process called sensory adaptation in response to a constant stimulus to ensure the brain isn't overloaded (Campbell et al., 2020).

Figure 2: Diagram showing how different magnitudes of stimulation affect the amount of action potentials produced (image taken from Campbell Biology textbook).

How Does it Reach the Visual Cortex?

The optic nerves A bunch of axons from ganglion cells weaved together, it is the pathway for action potentials to travel on to get to the visual cortex of both eyes meet at the optic chiasm, the space right before the cerebral cortex, and swap sides so the left side's stimulation goes to the right side of the brain (see figure 3 for a visualization) (Campbell et al., 2020).

The action potentials are carried to the visual cortex by synapsing with interneurons in the brain, jumping between the neurons. (Campbell et al., 2020).

Figure 3: Diagram showing how data from the right visual side combines to reach the left side of the brain, and the same concept for that of the left stimulation (image taken from Campbell Biology textbook)

What Happens in the Visual Cortex?

The visual cortex is located at the back of the brain, and it processes information from the retinas A structure that covers the entire back wall of the eye, it is filled with photoreceptors and assembles images in our heads.

When action potentials come into the brain, circuits of neurons process the input to create a perception of the stimuli, forming as colors, smells, sounds, and tastes. (Campbell et al., 2020).

Fun Fact: Colors, smells, sounds, and taste don't exist outside of the brain, it is all created in response to action potentials from the sensory receptors.
