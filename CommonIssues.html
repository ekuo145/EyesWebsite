Overview

Not everyone's eyes are able to see as well as the normal person.

Common issues with the eyes are myopia (nearsightedness), hyperopia (farsightedness) and colorblindness.

What is Myopia?

Myopia is the medical term for nearsightedness.

It is usually developed during childhood and adolescence (MayoClinic).

Compared to normal vision, which uses the lens Tooltip text and cornea Tooltip text to focus light on the retina Tooltip text, the eye of someone with myopia is much longer and oval shaped (see figure 1 above), resulting in light being focused in front of the retina instead (MayoClinic).

Some common risk factors include: genetics, doing a lot of up close activities like reading, screen time, and not going outside enough (MayoClinic)

Hyperopia and Presbyopia

Hyperopia is the medical term for farsightedness.

Presbyopia is the medical term for age-related farsightedness

People with farsightedness can see things clearly further away, but close up activities like reading get blurry

Hyperopia happens because the eye is too short or too flat (see figure 1 above), causing light to be underfocused and be focused past the retina, making it blurry (ClevelandClinic)

The main cause for this is genetic, and it develops when one is very little

Presbyopia is developed after 40 and continues to get worse until 65 (MayoClinic)

The lens gets less flexible as one gets older, so it struggles with adjusting to focus light correctly, sending the light past the retina (MayoClinic)

Colorblindness

Colorblindness is a condition that makes people have difficulties distinguishing between certain colors. It is often caused by a genetic defect that affects the cones.

Colorblindness usually runs in families and is passed down genetically (National Eye Institute).

The different types of colors can vary, with the most common being red-green and the less common complete colorblindness, when people don't see any color (see figure 2 below).

People at higher risk for color blindness are: Men, people with family history, people with certain eye diseases, people with diabetes/alzheimers/multiple scelosis, or are white (National Eye Institute)

What is Glaucoma

Glaucoma is a condition that causes blindness as a result of damage to the optic nerve (National Eye Institute).

It starts off with losing peripheral vision, and when not treated, can lead to blindness

Glaucoma can be caused a blockage of ducts that drain the aqueous humor (see figure 3 below), the build up of which will increase the pressure in the eye (Campbell Biology).

People at higher risk for glaucoma are: African American people older than 40, people older than 60, especially those who are Hispanic/Latino, and people with family history (National Eye Institute)
